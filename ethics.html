Ethical Reflections

Jamie Corstorphine 21155713

There are many ethical quandaries that arise from the use of stereo vision in self driving cars. Safety and privacy are two very important aspects to us that could be put at risk without us putting further development into the technologies and laws that we will be implementing. Firstly, the risk to human life that could be caused, without immense and detailed interrogation of the stereo vision technologies we are trying to use, is too high as is. Secondly, the gaping breach in privacy that we would be exposed to immediately if the technology was implemented at the moment would be astronomical. We have previously and currently experience what damage these sort of breaches in our privacy can do in our personal lives. As a result, there needs to be sweeping changes and regulations to be put in place before we move to a complete use of stereo vision on our roads.

The solution to the problem of safety with the use of stereo vision technology is fairly simple. The current issues that are active with the technology is well known and is constantly being worked on. When environments are unpredictable and the stereo vision cameras cannot perform as well as expected, other technologies to either enhance how they work or safety measures that could be implemented are currently being developed. The main issue with what is happening, is with the current use of these technologies on our roads. The information as to what needs to change to keep people safe is not completely available yet, but we are still using the technology on our roads. While arguments have been made regarding how the number of casualties due to faulty self-driving car technology are generally lower than regularly driven cars, the demographic of people who are currently using self driving car functions and have access to them is extremely small in comparison to manually driven car drivers. Overall, the statistics we are provided are skewed to favour good conditions for self-driving cars such as well lit urban or suburban areas or motorways and as such, stereo vision will generally be at its highest efficiency.

The second problem, however, will not be solved so easily. The ethical issues surrounding privacy of information has been a huge issue in the past and continues to be until today. Many people are unaware of the information that most large companies have access to and even more people are unaware of the negative effects of this. The increased commoditisation of our world has contributed to the largest amount of wealth inequality the world has ever seen and the use and sale of our information has pushed this greatly. When mobile phones were introduced, we were left exposed to the unforeseen attacks on privacy that have never been experienced before. Having a device that we willingly provided as much information to as possible without limiting the use of this information by third parties has left scars on our society that have not and probably will not heal from. The last thing we need is to expose ourselves and others a second time. Time needs to be given to properly introduce laws and regulations that took years to develop after mobile phones became widespread. Allowing self-driving cars to create another gap in our privacy should be avoided as much as possible.

Overall, self-driving cars and their use of stereo vision has the potential to drastically change our roads for the better. However, we are not at the stage where we can fully rely on them just yet. Fully embracing the technologies they use at the moment would leave us exposed to potential safety issues and also privacy issues that could negatively impact society for years to come. I suggest that the best course of action would be to focus on the two areas of developing the technologies to work under more strenuous environments and to also develop the legal safety nets required to stop the misuse of our information. We are almost there when it comes to self-driving cars that are reliable and helpful to society and we shouldn't be overeager to also have them damage our society.

Ryan Garcia

As bionic technology becomes more prevalent in the future ethical questions presented by the rapid development of this branch of medicine will have to be debated. Health and safety are significant issues. The functionality of a couple of clinical trials with human subjects or a few animal tests cannot reliably predict prostheses for the rest of someone's life. As a result, there is a real danger that people will be fitted with implants. Prostheses or implants that fail, have adverse side effects, or are even rejected by the body's autoimmune system, such as artificial hearts, have already demonstrated the body's resistance to technological advances. The ideal solution is to test the prosthesis over many years or decades and a large number of human test subjects. Problems with this are the extensive cost and experimental and clinical trials on humans as test subjects also raise ethical issues. Animal testing for prosthetics is not a worthy alternative which also presents problems.

Identity is the next ethical concern. Acquiring a prosthesis requires people to accept that a part of their body is artificial and that their biological work is dependent on a piece of technology. This may be intensified by bionic and neuroprosthetic implants, which may display or induce behaviours that are only partially controlled by us and with which we may struggle to identify. Moreover, the rapid development of cognitive prosthetics is a neuroprosthesis that cognitively aids function. This could directly undermine identity as they interface with the mind. Some opponents of prosthetics have argued for the integrity of the human body, with all of its flaws and defects, and have expressed concern that as we become more cyborgs, the essence of our humanity and social identity will be lost.

Ethical Reflections

Lachlan Pearce

The first point I want to make is whether or not a person should have the right to choose to have a bionic technology implanted in their body. Many people would say that a person should have the right to choose what happens to their body, even if it means choosing to have a bionic technology implanted. However, there are also people who would say that this type of technology should only be used when absolutely necessary, such as when a person has lost a limb.

The second point I would like to make is related to the first one and has to do with the safety of bionic technology. There are many people who are concerned about the safety of bionic technology and whether or not it is safe for humans to use. There have been some reports of people who have had bionic technology implants who have experienced complications, such as infections.

The third point I would like to make is about the long-term effects of bionic technology. There is not a lot of research on the long-term effects of bionic technology, so we don't really know what could happen to a person if they use this technology for an extended period of time. There are some people who are concerned that the long-term effects of bionic technology could be harmful to a person's health.

Lastly, overall I believe that helping disabled or impaired people with this technology is amazing and will be life-changing, but I am concerned about the long-term effects of the other uses of this tech enhancing people above human capabilities.

Cuong Nguyen

With the increasing usage of bionic people, there are several points I would like to address: The effect of bionic people in military/warfare, the change in the use of a current weapon to accommodate them with the rise of cybernetic augmentation and the shift in the employment process.

The first point is will the military force the soldiers to use artificial implantation such as eyes for scanning, aiming and legs for optimal movement. Will the implantation be given as a choice for the soldiers with the missing body? Will the soldiers get to keep the limbs after they retire or get a replacement that is more suitable for daily life?

Another point related to the military is the usage of current military technology. The increase of bionic people may add more effect to the current weapons. For example, the EMP (Electromagnetic pulse), at the moment, EMP does not affect organic lifeform. However, it can temporarily or permanently disable electrical and electronic equipment. The artificial implantation will receive heavy damage, and this can range from not working arms or legs to having the artificial organ fried inside you.

The last point is similar to the first point, but on a broader aspect, can the employer require the employee to have enhanced functions or favour the employee who possesses such functions?

Ryan Garcia

As bionic technology becomes more prevalent in the future ethical questions presented by the rapid development of this branch of medicine will have to be debated. Health and safety are significant issues. The functionality of a couple of clinical trials with human subjects or a few animal tests cannot reliably predict the functionality of a prosthesis for a person's lifespan. As a result, there is a real danger that people will be fitted with implants. Prostheses or implants that fail, have adverse side effects, or are even rejected by the body's autoimmune system, such as artificial hearts, have already demonstrated the body's resistance to technological advances. The ideal solution is to test the prosthesis over many years or decades and a large number of human test subjects. Problems with this are the extensive cost and experimental and clinical trials on humans as test subjects also raise ethical issues. Animal testing for prosthetics is not a worthy alternative which also presents problems.

Identity is the next ethical concern. Acquiring a prosthesis requires people to accept that a part of their body is artificial and that their biological work is dependent on a piece of technology. This may be intensified by bionic and neuroprosthetic implants, which may display or induce behaviours that are only partially controlled by the user and with which we may struggle to identify. Moreover, the rapid development of cognitive prosthetics to enhances cognitive function. This could directly undermine identity as they interface with the mind. Some opponents of prosthetics have argued for the integrity of the human body, with all of its flaws and defects have expressed concern that as we become more artificial, the essence of our humanity and social identity could be lost.

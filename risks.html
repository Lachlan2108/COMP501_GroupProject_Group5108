Risks

Bionic body parts are electronic devices that require computers and firmware to function. As modern prosthetics are becoming more accessible, there are ethical security risks from hackers or firmware bugs that can endanger people. Prosthetics can become a target for hackers to extort and control the wellbeing of an individual. Firmware bugs can also threaten users' health, such as the St. Jude's pacemaker, which had to be recalled as firmware bugs that caused the device to fail to maintain the user's heartbeat.

Despite the breakthroughs of artificial body parts from just being cosmetic to becoming functional or enhancing, it is still nowhere like how we imagine it like in sci-fi films. Bionic arms and legs are powered by batteries that need to be charged daily to function. There are limitations with our current technology on how effective the functionality of bionic prosthetics is.

There are also numerous potential issues of bone fractures, infection, comfort and implant stability which can significantly impact the user's wellbeing. These are just some common adverse effects if patients have the money and accessibility for the treatment.

Ethics

Identity

Receiving artificial implantation can have a significant effect on a person's identity. They have to accept that they have to rely on a piece of technology to maintain a level of independence. Therefore, this may have a detrimental effect on their self-image, causing them to view themselves as something less than a human. Some critics agree with this viewpoint, saying that with the increase of cybernetic enhancement, all the flaws and defects that make us human.

Culture

Artificial implantation not only has an effect on the people on who the procedure was performed but also on society as well. Some disabled communities view the risen of implantation as a threat to the continuance of the community. For example, the history and culture of the deaf community may end up being destroyed by the risen of cochlear implants.

Justice

With the development of artificial prostheses and implants, many questions regarding the issue of distributing justice: Will there be a division between the people who have artificial implants/prostheses and those who don't? Will all be able to access the implants that are customized to their biological traits and their way of life? Because the workings of machines will influence the behaviour or cognitive processes of their wearers, bionic and neuroprosthetic implants may raise problems of accountability. Who is to blame if such people cause accidents or make poor decisions: them or their implants?

Bionic Augmentation

Human augmentation's ethical implications. Human augmentation raises a number of ethical issues in addition to those mentioned previously. Is it ever morally acceptable to destroy or impair healthy human limbs or organs to accommodate an augmentation, especially if the destruction is irreversible? Can an employer require or place a premium on an employee's ability to perform enhanced functions? Part of the debate over human augmentation has centred on military applications, specifically the creation of super-soldiers. Should military research be devoted to developing a super-soldier through implants, integrated weaponry, genetically altered muscles and lightning-fast artificial nerves? If certain augmentations become very popular, there is a risk that they will be accepted as the standard, and people who do not have one will be considered disabled.
